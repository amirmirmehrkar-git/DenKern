# Domain Expert Candidate List — Hamburg Network

> **Purpose:** Identify the 10 strongest candidate profiles for the two Domain Expert interviews required to pass the Sprint 7 Gate.
>
> These are NOT customer discovery targets. These are independent validators — former manufacturing operations leaders, now free to speak without commercial agenda. The freight forwarder contacts in `42-hamburg-target-list-and-outreach.md` are potential customers. The candidates below are truth-tellers.

---

## The Critical Distinction

The existing Hamburg target list is freight forwarders (Rohlig, Senator, FMS). They are the right Tier 1 for customer discovery.

Domain Expert interviews require a different profile entirely:

| | Customer Discovery | Domain Expert Interview |
|---|---|---|
| Who | Current ops managers at logistics companies | Former Supply Chain / Operations Directors at manufacturers |
| Status | Active employees — guarded | Independent consultants or retired — speak freely |
| What they validate | Pain, willingness to pay, workflow fit | Organisational truth about outcome capture |
| What they cannot answer | How outcomes are really captured inside their org | — |
| Relevant to gate | No | **Yes — gates H1, H2, H3, H7, H9** |

---

## The Fastest Warm Path

Before searching cold, use the existing network.

**Ask your freight forwarder contacts (Rohlig, Senator, FMS ops managers):**

> "Who at your manufacturing customers would you say genuinely understood what it cost when a disruption went wrong? Is there someone who's left their company or retired who might talk to us?"

A senior ops manager at Rohlig has personally serviced 50+ manufacturing Supply Chain Directors. One warm introduction from them cuts weeks off sourcing time.

## Ranked Candidate Profiles

Rankings weight **learning value for H1–H9** (50%) and **accessibility from Hamburg network** (50%).

---

### #1 — Former Supply Chain Director, Beiersdorf (Hamburg HQ)

**Learning value:** ★★★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★★★

**Why top ranked:** Beiersdorf (NIVEA, La Prairie, Eucerin) is a Hamburg-headquartered FMCG company with deep Asia-Pacific import dependency via Hamburg port. The disruption profile maps directly to CASE-001.

**Hypothesis coverage:**
- H9 (10+ disruptions/year): FMCG supply chains run 30–50+ disruptions annually. Near-certain confirmation.
- H1 (outcome visible within 2 weeks): Retail shelf availability is visible within days of vessel arrival.
- H3 (natural close event): Order fulfillment / shelf replenishment is a clearly defined close trigger.
- H7 (ops owns financial outcome): FMCG companies typically have tight ops–P&L coupling.

**Risk:** Large company; Finance may be separated from Ops more than in Mittelstand.

**How to find:** LinkedIn/XING: "Head of Supply Chain Beiersdorf" → filter "Former". Beiersdorf has undergone multiple restructurings; several SC leaders now work independently. Handelskammer Hamburg alumni events. BME Hamburg chapter.

**Pitch note:** Former Beiersdorf SC leaders are often proud of the company and willing to give back to early-stage ventures from Hamburg.

### #2 — Former Supply Chain Director, Aurubis Hamburg

**Learning value:** ★★★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★★

**Why high ranked:** Aurubis is Hamburg's largest industrial employer — a copper smelter that imports 2M+ tonnes of copper concentrate annually via Hamburg port. The supply chain is 100% maritime-dependent. A single vessel delay directly stops production. Production downtime cost is in the range of €20,000–50,000/hour. This is the closest company-level match to the CASE-001 scenario that exists in Hamburg.

**Hypothesis coverage:**
- H1: Operational outcome (production stopped/continued) is known within hours. Financial outcome within days–weeks.
- H2: Copper concentrate pricing means Finance is closely involved in cost calculation — interesting H7 stress test.
- H7: In metals/commodities, Finance often owns the cost figure. This candidate may **challenge** H7. That is valuable — not all pilots will be FMCG.
- H9: Industrial production runs continuously; disruptions are frequent and documented.

**Risk:** Finance/Ops boundary may differ from target ICP. Treat as a valuable stress test for H7.

**How to find:** LinkedIn: "Supply Chain" OR "Operations" + "Aurubis" → filter "Former". Logistik Initiative Hamburg member directory. Hamburg metals industry association (WV Stahl). Warm intro via HHLA logistics contacts.

### #3 — Independent Supply Chain Consultant (ex-multiple Hamburg manufacturers)

**Learning value:** ★★★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★★★

**Why high ranked:** A consultant who has worked inside 5–10 North German manufacturing clients in the last 3–5 years can give you cross-company patterns that no single company employee can. One conversation can confirm or challenge hypotheses across multiple sectors simultaneously.

**Hypothesis coverage:** All of H1–H9. Cross-company evidence is more credible than single-company evidence for any hypothesis.

**How to find:**
- LinkedIn: Hamburg + "supply chain" + "Berater" OR "consultant" + manufacturing keywords.
- BVL Nord chapter (German Logistics Association, Hamburg): email the chapter directly asking for a senior member referral.
- Logistik Initiative Hamburg: member directory includes independent consultants.
- Search for people who speak at Hamburg logistics events (conference speaker lists for Logistikbranche Hamburg, BVL annual congress).

**Pitch note:** Consultants actively want to meet startups — this is business development for them. Easiest cold outreach of the entire list.

### #4 — Former Plant Manager, Airbus Hamburg (A320 family production)

**Learning value:** ★★★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★

**Why high ranked on learning value:** The A320 family is assembled in Hamburg-Finkenwerder. A single missing part stops an assembly line that costs Airbus €15–30M per aircraft in delayed delivery penalties. Production outcome is known in hours. Financial impact is the most precisely quantified of any industry on this list.

**Hypothesis coverage:**
- H1: Outcome known extremely fast — hours to days.
- H2: Cost data is accessible to ops (they carry budget responsibility for slot delays).
- H3: Natural close event is very clear (aircraft delivered to airline = case closed).
- H7: In aerospace, Ops owns the delivery commitment; Finance confirms the penalty calculation. Ops/Finance boundary is better defined than in most industries.

**Important caveat:** Airbus outcome capture is atypically rigorous (aerospace documentation standards). This candidate gives you the upper bound. Do not generalise to all manufacturing. Use this conversation to understand what the ideal looks like, then contrast with less-structured environments.

**How to find:** Hamburg Aviation network (www.hamburg-aviation.de) — direct outreach to the association. LinkedIn: "Airbus Hamburg" + "Plant Manager" OR "Werk" + "Former". Former Airbus managers often become aerospace consultants or join Tier-1 suppliers.

### #5 — Former Head of Procurement / Supply Chain, Tchibo Hamburg

**Learning value:** ★★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★★

**Why ranked:** Tchibo is Hamburg's most recognisable Mittelstand-origin company (now mid-large, still Hamburg-anchored). It imports coffee and consumer goods from 40+ countries with heavy Asia-Pacific maritime dependency. The seasonal production cycle means disruptions cluster around predictable windows — very high disruption frequency.

**Hypothesis coverage:**
- H9: Seasonal import cycles → multiple annual disruption events per product category.
- H3: Seasonal range launches are hard calendar events that create clear outcome close triggers.
- H1: Retail outcomes visible within the launch window (sold out = success; excess inventory = cost).

**Risk:** Consumer goods outcome logic differs from B2B industrial. Customer commitment in retail is about sell-through, not delivery contracts. Worth including to test whether the Outcome Model generalises.

**How to find:** Handelskammer Hamburg alumni program. LinkedIn: "Tchibo" + "Supply Chain" OR "Einkauf" → Former. Tchibo has restructured repeatedly; many senior SC figures now independent.

### #6 — Former Supply Chain Director, Jungheinrich (forklift manufacturing, Hamburg/Norderstedt)

**Learning value:** ★★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★

**Why ranked:** Jungheinrich is a German B2B industrial equipment manufacturer (forklifts, warehouse systems) headquartered near Hamburg. It manufactures at Norderstedt with complex component supply from Asia and Eastern Europe. Customer delivery commitments are contractually binding (B2B equipment delivery schedules are precise). This profile represents the Mittelstand manufacturing reality that is the target ICP.

**Hypothesis coverage:**
- H7: In Mittelstand manufacturing, Procurement/Ops typically owns the cost — Finance is a reporting layer, not a gatekeeper. Strong H7 confirmation candidate.
- H2: Cost data is accessible to Procurement/Ops in German Mittelstand — H2 likely confirmed.
- H9: Industrial equipment = fewer but more expensive disruptions. May be at the lower edge of H9.

**How to find:** VDMA Hamburg (German machinery manufacturers association) chapter. LinkedIn: "Jungheinrich" + "Supply Chain" OR "Logistics" → Former. Hamburg Mittelstand network.

### #7 — Former VP Operations, Helm AG (chemical distribution, Hamburg HQ)

**Learning value:** ★★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★

**Why ranked:** Helm AG is a global chemical distributor headquartered in Hamburg. Every shipment has a precise cost-per-tonne margin. Disruptions result in immediate, quantifiable financial consequences. Finance and Operations jointly own the outcome in commodity trading/distribution — this is the most valuable candidate to test the edge case of H7.

**Hypothesis coverage:**
- H2: Chemical traders have real-time cost visibility — outcome cost is known quickly.
- H7: Ops and Finance co-own the outcome in commodity distribution. Neither owns it exclusively. Potentially challenges the clean Ops-owns-it assumption.
- H1: Commodity outcomes are visible within days (spot market price movement tracks the disruption impact).

**Risk:** Chemical distribution is not manufacturing — it's closer to logistics than to operations. Treat this as an adjacent perspective, not a primary ICP confirmation.

**How to find:** VCI Hamburg (chemical industry association). LinkedIn: "Helm AG" + "Operations" OR "Supply Chain" → Former. Hamburg chemical sector network.

### #8 — Former Production or Operations Manager, Blohm+Voss / Lürssen Hamburg (shipbuilding)

**Learning value:** ★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★

**Why ranked:** Hamburg's historical identity is tied to shipbuilding. Blohm+Voss (now part of Lürssen) builds naval and luxury vessels at the Hamburg harbour. Supply chains are extremely complex (hundreds of subsystems per vessel). Outcome capture is contractually mandated in shipbuilding (delivery penalties are explicit in shipbuilding contracts).

**Hypothesis coverage:**
- H3: Very strong — delivery date is contractually defined; natural close event is vessel handover.
- H2: Financial impact is precisely calculated (contractual penalties are pre-defined).

**Important caveat:** Project timelines are 12–36 months. Outcome visibility for H1 (within 2 weeks) likely does NOT hold in shipbuilding — disruptions may cascade for months before outcome is clear. This candidate is most valuable for **challenging H1** rather than confirming it.

**Use case:** Include this profile specifically to stress-test H1. If operations managers in shipbuilding don't know the outcome within 2 weeks, the 7–14 day token TTL needs to be rethought for project-based industries. For the current ICP (repetitive disruptions, not project-based), the contrast is illuminating.

**How to find:** Hamburg shipbuilding association (VSM, Verband für Schiffbau und Meerestechnik). LinkedIn: Blohm+Voss OR Lürssen + Operations.

### #9 — Former Head of Supply Chain, Olympus Europa (medical devices, Hamburg HQ)

**Learning value:** ★★★ **Accessibility:** ★★★

**Why ranked:** Olympus Europa is headquartered in Hamburg. Medical devices have formally mandated outcome capture (ISO 13485, MDR compliance requires disruption documentation). This candidate shows you what mandatory, process-defined outcome capture looks like — valuable as a benchmark for what DenkKern could aspire to enable.

**Hypothesis coverage:**
- H2: Medical device supply chains have full cost visibility at Ops level (required for regulatory reporting).
- H3: Regulatory audit timelines create natural close events.

**Caveat:** Medical device outcome capture is atypically rigorous due to regulatory requirements. Will give you the upper-bound systematic view. Do not use to generalise about non-regulated manufacturing.

**How to find:** LinkedIn: "Olympus" + "Supply Chain" OR "Operations" → Hamburg → Former. Hamburg medtech/lifesciences network.

### #10 — Warm Referral via Existing Freight Forwarder Contacts

**Learning value:** ★★★★ (unknown until discovered) **Accessibility:** ★★★★★

**Why ranked:** This is not a company but a sourcing strategy. Nick's existing first-contact freight forwarder ops managers (Rohlig, Senator, FMS) have directly serviced dozens of manufacturing Supply Chain Directors. They know who made good decisions under pressure, who tracked outcomes rigorously, and who has since left their company.

**The ask:** After a customer discovery conversation ends:

> "One more thing — we're also trying to understand how outcome tracking actually works inside manufacturing companies after a disruption is resolved. Is there anyone you've worked with on the manufacturing side — ideally someone who's moved on from their company — who you think really understood that process? Someone we could have a 30-minute conversation with?"

This referral path has the highest accessibility of any item on this list and produces warm introductions rather than cold outreach.

**Who this typically surfaces:** Former supply chain or operations directors at local manufacturers who moved to consulting or advisory roles 1–3 years ago. Not yet visible on public lists but reachable in two hops.

## Outreach Template for Domain Expert Ask

This is different from the customer discovery message. The framing is explicitly research, not product validation:

> Subject: 30 minutes — how manufacturing teams track disruption outcomes
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm working with a Hamburg-based team building decision support for manufacturing operations. Before we design the next part of our product, we're trying to understand something very specific: how outcomes from supply disruption decisions are actually tracked inside manufacturing organisations — who knows what happened, when they know it, and where it gets recorded.
>
> We're not selling anything and we're not asking you to evaluate the product. We're looking for someone who's been directly responsible for these decisions and can tell us how they really worked in practice.
>
> Would you be open to a 30-minute conversation? I can work around your schedule.
>
> [Nick]

This message works because it is honest, specific, and asks for almost nothing (30 minutes, not 60). Upgrade to 60 if they engage.
